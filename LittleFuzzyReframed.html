Project Description

This project reimagines H. Beam Piper’s 1962 science fiction novel "Little Fuzzy", transforming its narrative into an interactive exploration of perspective and storytelling. In the original novel, events unfold through the viewpoints of various characters, each interpreting the world differently. For this project, the text has been adapted to fit the distinct points of view of characters such as Jack Holloway and Leonard Kellogg, highlighting how the same events can be perceived and understood in diverse ways.

The interface lets users drag and drop tiles representing scenes from the book into slots to construct their own version of the story. Each tile shows a scene from one of two characters’ perspectives, highlighting how tone, meaning, and emotional impact change depending on who is “telling” the story.

By rearranging tiles and switching perspectives, users can see firsthand how narrative is not fixed but relative and interpretive. Once satisfied, users can download their story as a PDF to save their creation, read it and learn one of the versions, or reset the tiles to experiment with new arrangements.

The project turns "Little Fuzzy" into an interactive exploration, showing that even a familiar story can feel new when viewed from different angles. It emphasizes that perspective is not just a detail of narration — it is a central force that shapes understanding and experience.
